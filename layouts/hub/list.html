How to Choose the Right {{ .Params.category_name }}

By Room Size

The size of your room should drive your choice more than anything else. As a rule of thumb: small rooms (under 150 sq ft) need a single focused light source; medium rooms work best with two light sources; large rooms need layered lighting from three or more sources. Choose {{ lower .Params.category_name }} proportionally — an oversized piece in a small room overwhelms; a tiny piece in a large room disappears.

By Existing Furniture Style

Your {{ lower .Params.category_name }} should complement the dominant material in your space. Gold and brass finishes pair best with warm wood tones and velvet upholstery. Chrome and nickel work with cooler greys and whites. Mixed metal? Match the finish to the largest piece of furniture in the room.

By Budget

See the price guide section below for a full breakdown of what you get at each budget tier. Short version: $50–$150 is the sweet spot for quality without overspending; $150–$500 is where you start to see real material and craftsmanship differences; $500+ is true investment-grade décor.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Cheap plastic parts painted to look like metal — check product listings for "ABS plastic base"
  • No UL or ETL safety listing — all electrical items should be safety certified
  • Shades that don't diffuse light evenly — look for fabric or frosted glass, not clear plastic
  • Cord length under 5 feet — always check cord length for floor and table lamps

Sizing + Placement Guide

Getting the size right is the single most common mistake buyers make. Here are the rules that interior designers use:

Table lamp height
The bottom of the shade should sit at eye level when you're seated — typically 58–64" from the floor
Floor lamp height
58–64" total height for reading; 72–78" for ambient or arc lamps
Pendant height
30–36" above a dining table; 7 feet minimum above a walkway
Chandelier width
Add the room's length + width in feet; that number in inches = ideal chandelier diameter
Lampshade width
Shade width should be ≈ twice the width of the lamp base

Materials + Quality Guide

Gold, Brass, and Chrome — What's the Difference?

Polished gold is a finish (plating over a base metal), bright and consistent. It suits maximalist glam interiors. Brass is a solid alloy with a warmer, living tone that develops a natural patina — more character, slightly more expensive. Chrome is cooler and more reflective — better for modern or art deco aesthetics than traditional glam.

Crystal Types

K9 crystal is the standard for décor lamps — optical-quality glass that refracts light beautifully. Asfour crystal is Egyptian-made and considered premium. Swarovski is the luxury tier. Acrylic crystals are budget alternatives — fine from a distance, but lack the genuine sparkle of real crystal up close.

Shade Materials

Fabric shades (linen, silk, velvet) give the warmest, most diffused light — ideal for glam and cosy aesthetics. Glass shades (clear, frosted, or seeded) create more defined light pools. Avoid thin plastic shades that yellow over time or create hot spots.

What Signals Quality

  • Weight — quality lamps are heavy (stable base, real materials)
  • UL/ETL safety certification listed in the product specs
  • Solid metal vs hollow-sounding when tapped
  • Smooth, consistent finish with no visible seams
  • Fabric shade with a plastic or metal liner inside

Care + Maintenance

Cleaning Crystal

Turn off and unplug first — always. Remove individual crystal drops if the design allows. Mix distilled water with white vinegar (50/50) in a spray bottle. Spray onto a microfibre cloth (not directly on the lamp) and wipe each crystal individually. Dry immediately with a clean cloth to prevent water marks. Do this 2–4 times per year.

Polishing Gold + Brass Finishes

For polished gold: wipe with a dry microfibre cloth weekly to prevent fingerprint buildup. Use a dedicated metal polish (e.g. Brasso for brass, Renaissance Wax for plated gold) every 6 months. Avoid abrasive cloths or bathroom cleaners — they strip plating permanently.

Bulb Replacement

Always check the maximum wattage rating on the shade or socket before replacing. Most glam lamps are rated for 40–60W equivalent. With LEDs, you can safely use a 9–10W LED to replace a 60W incandescent. Use warm white (2700K) for the most flattering glam effect.

Storage

If storing a crystal lamp: remove and individually wrap each crystal drop in tissue paper before boxing. Store shades upright, never stacked. Keep in a dry space — humidity causes tarnishing on metal bases.

Expected Lifespan

A quality glam lamp with proper care should last 10–15 years. The first component to fail is usually the wiring or socket — both can be replaced by an electrician for a fraction of the cost of a new lamp. Replace vs repair if: the base is cracked, the plating is peeling over more than 30% of the surface, or crystals are chipped at the top where they attach.

Lighting Basics — What Every Buyer Should Know

Lumens vs Watts

Watts measure energy use; lumens measure actual brightness. With LED bulbs now standard, watts are almost meaningless as a brightness guide. Instead: 400–500 lumens = soft bedside light; 800–1100 lumens = comfortable reading light; 1500+ lumens = bright task or ambient lighting. Most glam table lamps are designed for the 800–1100 lumen range.

Colour Temperature

Measured in Kelvin (K). For glam interiors: 2700K (warm white) is the gold standard — it makes gold and crystal finishes glow and creates the warm, luxurious atmosphere associated with upscale hotels. Avoid 5000K+ (daylight) bulbs in any decorative lamp — they make gold look greenish and kill the ambience.

Layered Lighting — The Designer Approach

The difference between a room that looks designed and one that doesn't is almost always lighting layers. Layer 1: ambient (overhead, general). Layer 2: task (table lamp, reading lamp). Layer 3: accent (sconces, LED strips, candles). Each layer should be on a separate switch or dimmer. A well-layered room can be set to any mood — bright and energising for daytime, warm and intimate for evenings.

Dimmer Compatibility

Not all LED bulbs are dimmable — check the package. If installing a dimmer switch, buy dimmable LEDs specifically. The best glam lamps use E26 or E12 base sockets, both of which have a wide selection of dimmable LED options. Avoid smart bulbs in decorative lamps unless you want the visible bulb to be part of the aesthetic.

Price Guide — What to Expect at Every Budget

Under $50

Entry-level decorative lamps — primarily from mass-market retailers. Expect ABS plastic bases with a metallic paint finish, basic fabric or paper shades, and standard sockets. Good for: rentals, children's rooms, or testing a style before committing. Not suitable for a main living or bedroom statement piece.

$50 – $150 ★ Sweet Spot

The best value tier for most buyers. You start to see real metal bases (zinc alloy, aluminium), K9 crystal accents, lined fabric shades, and proper cord management. Brands at this level typically have UL certification and longer warranties. This is where 80% of our featured picks live.

$150 – $500

Solid construction, genuine materials (real brass, hand-blown glass, premium crystal), and noticeably better finishing. Lamps at this level become room focal points — they're designed to be looked at, not just functional. Expect 5+ year lifespans with proper care.

$500+

Investment-grade decorative lighting. Heirloom materials (solid brass, Swarovski or Asfour crystal, hand-sewn silk shades), designer names, and bespoke finishes. Buy once, keep forever. These are the pieces that become part of a home's identity.
